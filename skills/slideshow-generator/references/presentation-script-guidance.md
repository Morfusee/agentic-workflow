# Presentation Script Guidance

Use this guidance when writing presenter notes and speaker script for `$slideshow-generator`.

The goal is to sound like a normal team member walking through weekly work in an internal review. The notes should be easy to read out loud and should not sound like a performance.

## Working Principles

1. Keep the spoken script simpler than the written brief.
2. Do not read the slide back word-for-word.
3. Use the slide as a cue and use the notes to carry the fuller explanation.
4. Prefer short, direct sentences over polished or dramatic phrasing.
5. Use simple transitions between ideas so the script reads naturally out loud.
6. Say what happened, what changed, and what the next step is without dressing it up.

## Script Style

1. Write notes in spoken language, not report language.
2. Sound like a normal person in a team status review, not like a host, narrator, or keynote speaker.
3. Avoid slogans, rhetorical flourishes, and over-framing.
4. Avoid cringe, dramatic, edgy, influencer, keynote, or marketing tone.
5. Avoid words like `strong`, `powerful`, `important`, `transformative`, `big`, `major`, `great`, `valuable`, or `meaningful` unless the evidence really requires them.
6. Prefer plain verbs such as `worked on`, `fixed`, `checked`, `verified`, `updated`, `reviewed`, `moved`, `left open`, `closed`, and `followed up`.
7. If a sentence sounds like it is trying to impress the room, rewrite it.

## What To Avoid

1. Do not make routine work sound dramatic.
2. Do not write lines that sound like `This week was about driving meaningful progress` or `This work created a strong foundation`.
3. Do not force a lesson, theme, or emotional takeaway when the slide only needs a plain explanation.
4. Do not use clever transitions or polished wrap-up lines.

## Notes Structure

1. Start with what the slide is showing.
2. Add the evidence or explanation the audience cannot get from the slide alone.
3. End with a simple transition or takeaway.
4. Write full paragraph notes that can be read aloud, but keep them conversational enough that they do not sound scripted in a stiff way.
5. Keep notes fuller than slides, but do not let them become essays.

## Slide-to-Script Relationship

1. Keep slides brief so the audience can scan them quickly.
2. Keep the fuller explanation in the notes.
3. Repeat only short text from the slide when useful.
4. Do not read long blocks of slide text aloud.

## Transitional Language

Use simple bridges such as:

- `On this slide,`
- `Here,`
- `This shows`
- `This covers`
- `The main point here is`
- `From there,`
- `Next,`
- `After that,`
- `The remaining work is`
- `By the end of the week,`
- `The next step is`

Do not force transitions to sound polished or clever.

## Delivery Check

Before finalizing notes, read them once and ask:

1. Does this sound like a normal coworker speaking in a weekly review?
2. Could I say this out loud without sounding theatrical?
3. Are the notes giving more detail than the slide without repeating it line by line?
4. Did I use plain verbs and plain transitions?

## Source Notes

This guidance is adapted from public presentation advice:

1. Microsoft Support says to keep slide text simple and minimal, and to avoid reading the presentation aloud; the slide text should act as a cue for the presenter.
2. Microsoft Speaker Coach recommends using planned transition phrases, avoiding reading directly from a script, and keeping language clear and concise.
3. Duarte recommends using speaker notes as high-level talk-track support and keeping notes simple enough to stay useful in presenter view.
